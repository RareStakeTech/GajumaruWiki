# QPQ Monetary Philosophy

The theoretical foundation of Gajumaru's monetary design, spanning Austrian economics, historical monetary analysis, and the explicit design rationale for Gaju's properties. Primarily sourced from the extended discussion in [[summary-video-bitcoin-proof-of-concept]].

---

## The Austrian Economics Foundation

QPQ explicitly grounds its monetary thinking in the Austrian economics tradition — primarily Ludwig von Mises and Friedrich Hayek — and their concept of **"real money"**: a commodity whose supply is governed only by physics, not politics.

**Real money formal definition** (Craig's articulation in the Bitcoin PoC video):
> "Money based upon a finite resource that can be proven, whose extraction has a value — i.e. gold or a cryptographic puzzle — not debaseable, not censorable, tradable between peers without intermediation."

The five properties of real money:
1. **Finite** — supply is bounded by physics or mathematics, not decree
2. **Provable** — anyone can verify the quantity and authenticity
3. **Costly to produce** — extraction requires real effort (gold mining; computational work)
4. **Not debaseable** — no party can inflate the supply arbitrarily
5. **Peer-to-peer** — transferable without requiring a trusted intermediary

Gold historically satisfied all five. Fiat satisfies none after it was decoupled from gold.

## Historical Monetary Analysis

### The 2% Inflation Target — Where It Came From

The conventional 2% central bank inflation target traces to a simple historical observation: gold supplies grew at approximately **2% per year** through terrestrial mining. Modern central banks inherited this as the "natural" monetary growth rate — the rate at which new commodity money entered circulation. Stripped of gold backing, the 2% target became arbitrary permission to inflate without discipline.

Craig's sharper framing: central banks "conflated the meaning of price increases with inflation, saying 'well, we can actually do inflation as long as we pass it to the right people because they won't spill it out into the public economy where the CPI would go up.'" The 2% target became cover for managed inflation passed through capital markets before reaching consumer prices.

### JP Morgan's 1912 Congressional Testimony

Craig references JP Morgan testifying before the United States Congress in 1912 on the question of what money actually is. Morgan — architect of American banking — described the greenback (effectively what fiat money is today) as "not money... a credit note."

This is not an Austrian fringe position. The person who designed the US financial system understood the categorical difference between money and a promise-to-pay. The modern financial system subsequently chose credit notes over real money.

### David Graeber and the 5,000-Year Counter-Claim

David Graeber's *Debt: The First 5,000 Years* argues that **credit-money** — not commodity-money — has been the dominant form of exchange throughout human history. Graeber's thesis: money as physical commodity is a myth; debt and credit relationships predate coinage.

Greg's position is the direct counter: Greg explicitly says (in the Bitcoin PoC transcript) that his phrase "the next 5,000 years" is **"my little dig at Daniel Greyber"** — a deliberate counter-claim. Where Graeber looks back 5,000 years and sees credit-money, Greg is claiming Gajumaru will build **non-credit, peer-to-peer, commodity-equivalent money** for the *next* 5,000 years.

The debate is not merely academic — it determines what you build. If Graeber is right (credit-money is foundational), then stablecoins and CBDCs are natural. If the Austrian position is right (real money requires physics-equivalent constraint), then only PoW-based systems qualify.

## Gold as a One-Way Function (Metastability)

Craig introduces a physics analogy to explain why gold became real money:

Gold is **metastable** — it has physically passed through a one-way function within the universe. Gold nuclei were forged in supernovae and neutron star collisions (stellar nucleosynthesis). You cannot reverse this process on Earth. The energy required to recreate a gold nucleus from lighter elements is cosmologically large. Gold extraction from the Earth is therefore a proxy for accessing this irreversible physical process — you expend real energy to recover something that the universe produced through incomprehensible violence.

Cryptographic mining creates a **mathematical one-way gateway** with equivalent properties:
- Finding a valid proof-of-work requires real computational energy
- The result (the hash) cannot be reversed to recover the input without re-doing the work
- This irreversibility is mathematical rather than physical — but equally absolute

This is Satoshi's deep insight: **a cryptographic puzzle can stand in for gold as the physics-based constraint on money creation**.

## Why Gold Will Fail in the Space Age

Craig identifies a structural vulnerability in gold as the long-term monetary base:

> "There are trillions of tons of gold just floating around in space."

Asteroid mining will eventually make it possible to retrieve gold from metal-rich asteroids. At that point, the terrestrial constraint on gold supply collapses — the 2% annual growth rate could become 2,000%. This is not a distant theoretical: Craig explicitly frames Elon Musk's space exploration ambitions as primarily motivated by asteroid mining: "That's why dear brother Musk is exploring space — not because it's entertaining. It's because he thinks he can get a hold of a few asteroids and mine them."

A cryptographic puzzle has no such vulnerability. Mathematical scarcity is not bounded by planetary geology. For a currency intended to function across the interplanetary human species over 5,000 years, a cryptographic puzzle is structurally superior to gold.

## "Humanity as Transmission Fluid"

Greg's **DC stage** formulation of the current monetary system's distributional effect:

> "The reality is that humanity has been turned into the transmission fluid to slow the motion of monetary inflation into CPI and PPI. We're the squishy bit in the middle that can be compressed."

Monetary inflation does not translate immediately into consumer price increases (CPI) or producer prices (PPI). It first moves through financial assets, real estate, and corporate balance sheets — compressing the purchasing power of wages before prices visibly rise. The individual worker absorbs the monetary shock before it reaches the measured economy.

This is the **statists/corporists/individual** three-way tension:
- **Statists** hold the licence to print money; use inflation to manage outcomes
- **Corporatists** hold the licence to tax via fragmented infrastructure; extract value through intermediation
- **The individual** is squeezed between both; no effective exit in conventional finance

Gajumaru's claim is that a governance-free resource layer is the only mechanism that gives the individual an exit. See [[internet-of-economics]] for the full frame.

Greg cites the IMF as the institutional driver of a structural dynamic that requires no external enemy:

> "The IMF is very busy driving the agenda that we should continue to create public debt. Debt to whom? By whom? For whom? I have no idea. Neither do they."

This is the context for the 1 trillion Gaju supply figure. Craig's direct statement: "Why are there one trillion gajus? Well, why is there $100 trillion worth of private debt in the world?" The Gaju supply is calibrated to global economic scale — not to Bitcoin's 21-million ceiling designed for a niche store-of-value.

## The Self-Referential Currency

Gaju is designed to be **self-referential** — its purchasing power is expressed in real goods, not in dollar terms:

> "Somebody who gets on the tube in London doesn't try to pay in dollars — they pay in pounds and they don't try to think 'oh well £1.50 is actually $2.40.' Nobody does that."

Stablecoins are permanently dollar-defined — they reference fiat, making them derivative instruments rather than currencies. Gaju is designed to eventually stand alone as a unit of account: "how many eggs, how many pens" — not "how many dollars."

Greg explicitly frames Gajumaru as fulfilling the Bitcoin whitepaper's stated purpose:
> "We've done something from scratch with the Gaju. It's the only open global currency that delivers the vision that was originally proposed in the Bitcoin white paper."

The Bitcoin whitepaper's title is *"Bitcoin: A Peer-to-Peer Electronic Cash System."* Greg observes that people who cite Bitcoin as their inspiration at conferences "proceed to go on as if the title of the paper didn't exist." QPQ's claim: Gajumaru is the actual peer-to-peer electronic cash system the whitepaper described.

## Crypto as a Confidence Game

Craig's South Sea Bubble (1711) analogy:
- Real premise: foreign trade *was* going to be transformative
- Corruption: rampant speculation and fraud on top of that real premise
- Bitcoin and Ethereum = the real premise; the current crypto landscape = the fraud layer on top

The "market despair" dynamic is identified as distinct from traditional Ponzi dynamics: Craig observes that many people now enter crypto **consciously knowing it's a casino** — not because they're fooled, but because they see no legitimate path forward in the conventional economy (the "Joe Truck Driver" narrative). This is a structural symptom of monetary failure, not individual irrationality.

**Stablecoin framing** (from [[summary-video-stablecoins-genius-act]]): stablecoins are "a trust-me we have this money in reserve" claim — the word "con" derives from "confidence." The entire crypto value stack rests on narrative, not utility.

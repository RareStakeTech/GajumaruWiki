# Summary: Token Summit Liechtenstein — Gregory Chew on the Path to Decentralization

Greg Chew presented at the Token Summit in Liechtenstein alongside a moderator and co-panelist (Martin). A panel discussion on decentralization under MiCA and the path from centralized entities to decentralized infrastructure.

---

## Greg's 23-Year Journey

Greg introduced himself as having spent **23 years** pursuing the Internet of Economics:

> "I've been in this journey now for 23 years trying to achieve what I refer to as the Internet of Economics which is the restitution to the individual and the communities in which they reside both their data and their economic capacity."

He originally built a **centralized system** that attempted to unite major global markets into a single platform. The problem: it was a massive single point of failure. No major actor would join because of this concentration risk. He shelved it in **2009**, "believing that a technology would come that would open the door."

He then watched DLT projects receive "hundreds of millions of dollars" and assumed they would actually build a blockchain that worked. They did not. So QPQ assembled a team and built it themselves.

---

## QPQ as a Centralized Entity

> "QPQ is a centralized entity. I'm a majority shareholder of the company. So it's clearly centralized. But the Gajimaru is the blockchain that we have built to go out into that."

This is QPQ's answer to the decentralization question: QPQ the company is centralized and accountable; Gajumaru the protocol is intentionally decentralized at the resource layer. These are separate things.

---

## Cost Disclosure

> "We've spent 60 million to get to this point and by the way we're going to make the whole thing free and open to everybody. That's a hard sell, right?"

$60 million is the disclosed cost of building Groot and the product suite to MainNet.

---

## Decentralization Is a Process

Co-panelist/moderator framing (which Greg affirmed): "Decentralization is not a point in time, it's a process. It's an evolution. Nothing was started truly decentralized — not even the big bang."

Greg's practical response: QPQ went "the opposite direction" from the rest of the industry. From testnet and whitelist mining onward, the design ensures there would be **no governance of any kind at the resource layer**.

> "You cannot trust me. You can never ever — even if I were the Lord Jesus Christ himself — you cannot give me the keys to that kingdom."

---

## Tony Benn's Five Questions

Greg cited Tony Benn's five questions to power (from Hansard) as the universal test for any blockchain governance claim:

1. What power do you have?
2. How do you use it?
3. To what ends?
4. Who gave you that power?
5. How do we take it back?

> "If we can't depose you — if we can't take it back — then actually your power is absolute."

Layer 2s, DAOs with insider-controlled token voting, and PoS systems with identified foundations all fail questions 3–5.

---

## Whitelist Mining Purpose

> "We thought we could use whitelist mining to try and bring 100,000 people from Switzerland, Liechtenstein, Netherlands — our home spots as it were — and actually get them mining because we implemented the Cuckoo Cycle that allows it to be done with CPUs."

The whitelist mining period was designed to broaden the miner base before public opening, specifically recruiting from the project's home regions to achieve geographic decentralization.

---

## MiCA Recital 22

The moderator framed the discussion around MiCA Recital 22: if a crypto asset service provider is **fully decentralized**, they do not fall under MiCA regulation. The distinction: a "recital" describes the spirit of the law; an "article" is the actual law. Recital 22 opens the door to decentralized projects being exempt if they can demonstrate genuine decentralization.

Greg's position: GajuDEX is designed to pass this test. Gajumaru the protocol is designed to pass this test. But the path to proving it requires honest analysis, not claims.

---

## Independent Rating Agency Proposal

Greg proposed that a rating agency specializing in decentralization — similar to S&P/Moody's for creditworthiness — would be valuable. Such an agency could provide intelligent, measurable analysis of decentralization that regulators could rely on without having to be technically grounded themselves.

> "A rating agency that understood decentralization, understood how to impose those measures but also understand the intent and purpose and the spirit of what was being worked on from the other side would actually be extremely useful."
